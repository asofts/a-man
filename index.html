Things that will haunt you

01
Physics

You are mostly empty space

If you removed all the empty space from every atom in every human on Earth, all of humanity would fit into a sugar cube.

Atoms are 99.9999999999996% empty space. The nucleus is to an atom what a fly is to a cathedral. The "solid" table you're resting on isn't solid — it's electromagnetic forces giving the illusion of solidity.

02
Mathematics

Some infinities are bigger than others

Not all infinities are equal. The infinity of real numbers is provably, mathematically larger than the infinity of whole numbers.

Georg Cantor proved this in 1874. Between 0 and 1 alone, there are more real numbers than there are integers in all of infinity. He called different sizes of infinity "cardinalities." His contemporaries thought he was insane. He died in an asylum. He was right.

03
Biology

You are a community, not an individual

Your body contains roughly 38 trillion bacteria — slightly more than your own human cells. You are, technically, a collective organism.

These microbes influence your mood (via the gut-brain axis), your immune system, your weight, and possibly your personality. When you feel "like yourself," a significant portion of that self is microbial. The line between "you" and "not you" is genuinely blurry.

04
Philosophy

The Ship of Theseus never ended

If every plank of a ship is replaced over time, is it still the same ship? The ancient Greeks asked this. We still can't answer it.

Your body replaces most of its atoms every few years. Are you the same person you were at 8? The Sugababes replaced every original member — yet continued as a band. Bands, corporations, nations, and selves all face this problem. Identity may be a story we tell, not a fact we discover.

05
Mathematics

The Monty Hall Problem

Three doors. One car. You pick one. The host reveals a goat behind another. Should you switch? Yes — always. It doubles your odds.

Switching wins 2/3 of the time. Thousands of mathematicians wrote furious letters insisting this was wrong. It took computer simulations to convince them. The lesson: human intuition about probability is systematically, profoundly broken. Context changes probability. Always.

06
Physics

Time moves slower when you move faster

A clock on a fast-moving rocket ticks slower than one on Earth. This isn't metaphor — GPS satellites must correct for it or your maps drift by miles per day.

Einstein's special relativity is not theoretical — it's embedded in technology. Astronaut Scott Kelly returned from a year in space 8.6 milliseconds younger than his twin brother. Time dilation is real, measurable, and absolutely wild when you sit with it.

07
History

Cleopatra is closer to us than to the pyramids

Cleopatra lived around 30 BC. The pyramids were built around 2560 BC. She is historically closer to the Moon landing than to their construction.

The pyramids are so ancient that woolly mammoths were still alive when they were built. Our intuition for deep time is catastrophically bad. History isn't a single thread — it's geological layers. Ancient Rome and ancient Egypt barely overlap. Shakespeare and the Jamestown colony are contemporaries.

08
Psychology

You make decisions before you know it

Brain scans show your brain decides to move your hand up to 10 seconds before you consciously "decide" to. Free will may be a story told after the fact.

The Libet experiment and its successors suggest that conscious intention may be a post-hoc narrative — your brain acts, then you experience the feeling of deciding. This doesn't necessarily eliminate free will, but it radically reframes what it could possibly mean.

09
Philosophy

Why does anything exist at all?

Leibniz called it the fundamental question of metaphysics: why is there something rather than nothing? No one has ever answered it. Not even close.

Every scientific explanation assumes the existence of something — laws, particles, fields — to explain something else. But why do those things exist? You can't explain existence with existence. This may be permanently outside the reach of human knowledge. Sit with that.
